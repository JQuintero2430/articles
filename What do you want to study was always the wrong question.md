# “What do you want to study?” was always the wrong question

There’s a moment in almost everyone’s life that is absurdly important—and at the same time, absurdly neglected.

It’s that moment when you’re seventeen or eighteen, finishing high school, and someone—your mom, your dad, an uncle, or a career counselor who met you for twenty minutes—looks you in the eye and asks a question that sounds harmless:

**“So, what do you want to study?”**

You answer something.
Economics. Law. Medicine.

And everyone nods, as if you just solved a problem. As if that sentence, said without having worked a single day in your life, were a valid contract with your future.

And the unsettling part is: in many cases, it is.

Because most people who choose a career at that age will spend 60–70% of every working day of their adult life doing that activity. Every Tuesday. Every Wednesday. Every ordinary Thursday for the next forty years.

And no one frames it that way.

---

## The problem isn’t the choice. It’s the question.

The problem isn’t that young people choose poorly.

The problem is that the question itself is wrong from the start.

When you ask a seventeen-year-old “what do you want to study?”, they’re not hearing:

**“What kind of activity do you want to do every day?”**

What they’re actually hearing is:

**“What label do you want to wear?”**

And those two things are worlds apart.

A teenager might say, “I want to study economics because I like money.”
Sounds reasonable. But they’re not imagining what an economist actually does.

They’re not picturing eight hours in Excel, reports, models, uncomfortable meetings. They’re imagining the highlight. The cinematic version.

---

## Your brain isn’t built to decide this

Here’s where psychology comes in.

Researchers like Daniel Gilbert and Timothy Wilson showed that we’re terrible at predicting how we’ll feel in the future. They called this **affective forecasting**.

We systematically overestimate both the intensity and the duration of our future emotions.

We also suffer from **focalism**: when we imagine the future, we focus only on the main event and ignore everything else.

The future doctor imagines the brilliant diagnosis—not the 36-hour shifts.

The future lawyer imagines the courtroom victory—not the endless paperwork.

We’re making decisions based on incomplete simulations.

---

## And your “hardware” isn’t even ready

It gets worse.

The part of the brain responsible for long-term planning—the prefrontal cortex—doesn’t fully develop until around age 25.

Neuroscientist Laurence Steinberg explains this with the dual systems model: during adolescence, the emotional system is fully active, but the control system is still under construction.

In other words:

**We’re asking someone to make a 40-year decision with a brain that’s not fully built yet.**

And on top of that, they’re doing it under social pressure.

---

## You decide with whatever is available

There’s another layer.

According to Daniel Kahneman, most of our decisions are made by **System 1**: fast, automatic, intuitive.

When a teenager answers what they want to study, they’re not running a deep analysis. They’re just pulling from what’s available:

- what they’ve seen in movies
- what they’ve heard at home
- what sounds prestigious

That’s the **availability heuristic**.

There are thousands of careers a teenager never even considers—not because they’re bad, but because they don’t exist in their mental map.

---

## The first answer becomes an anchor

Then comes another bias: the **anchoring effect**.

Once someone says “I want to be an engineer,” that statement becomes the reference point for everything else.

From that moment on, every option is judged as:

“Is this better or worse than engineering?”

There’s no real exploration—only comparison.

---

## Commitment without exploration: the worst-case scenario

Psychologist James Marcia described this clearly.

He identified four identity states, and the most problematic is **foreclosure**:

Committing to an identity without exploring alternatives.

Which is exactly what the question “what do you want to study?” pushes people to do.

It demands commitment before exploration.

---

## Philosophy: the problem runs deeper

This isn’t just a practical mistake. It’s an existential one.

Jean-Paul Sartre argued that _existence precedes essence_: we are not born to be something—we become something through what we do.

Choosing a professional label to escape uncertainty is what he called **bad faith**.

Martin Heidegger described how people live in “the They”: doing what “one does,” without questioning it.

And Søren Kierkegaard captured the tension perfectly:

> Life can only be understood backwards, but it must be lived forwards.

Choosing a career at 18 is exactly that.

---

## The right question changes everything

So what’s the right question?

Not:

**“What do you want to study?”**

But:

**“What kind of daily life are you willing to live?”**

This shift is profound.

Because now you can’t answer with a label.

You have to imagine concrete actions:

- Sitting in front of a screen for 8 hours
- Talking to people all day
- Solving technical problems in silence
- Moving constantly

Daily activity points to the career—not the other way around.

---

## Aristotle figured this out 2,400 years ago

More than two millennia ago, Aristotle already had the answer.

**Eudaimonia**—human flourishing—is not a feeling. It’s an activity.

You don’t become fulfilled by what you are.
You become fulfilled by what you consistently do.

---

## The data isn’t encouraging

Reality backs this up:

- Only a minority work in fields related to their degree
- University dropout rates are high across Latin America
- Most people feel disengaged from their work

The issue isn’t the career itself.

It’s that it was never a real choice.

---

## Choosing a career is choosing your rock

Albert Camus said it in a way that’s hard to forget.

Life is repetition:

wake up, commute, work, eat, sleep… repeat.

Choosing a career is choosing what you’ll repeat for decades.

It’s choosing your rock.

And like in the myth of Sisyphus, meaning isn’t found at the top.

It’s found in the pushing.

---

## So what should you do?

The honest answer is uncomfortable:

“I don’t know yet.”

But that’s not a problem.

It’s the correct starting point.

Because what matters isn’t knowing what title you want.

It’s discovering **what kind of days you want to live**.

---

In the end, the truth is simpler—and harder—than it seems:

**You are not your title.
You are what you do every Tuesday at 10 a.m.**
