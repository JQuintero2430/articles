There’s something off… and I think most people feel it, but don’t really have the language for it.
Like there are fewer and fewer places where you can just… exist.
Not perform, not consume, not optimize your time. Just be there.
And it’s not nostalgia. It’s not that vague “things used to be better” feeling. It’s more structural than that.
This is third places, I’m Jorge and you’re hitting the button to follow this channel.
The idea itself is almost too simple. Ray Oldenburg came up with it decades ago. Your life is split into three spaces: home, work… and then this third place that is neither.
That in-between space where you’re not required to be productive, and you’re not required to be close to anyone either. You just show up. You see familiar faces. Maybe you talk. Maybe you don’t.
Cafés, parks, libraries… that kind of thing.
But the physical place is not really the point.
What matters is what happens inside it.
Oldenburg kept coming back to this one idea: conversation is the main activity. Not a side effect… the core.
And if you pause for a second… it gets uncomfortable pretty quickly.
How many places exist today where conversation is actually the point… and not just something that happens while you’re doing something else?
…
There’s something the UNESCO texts say that sounds a bit dramatic at first. They describe these spaces as essential to democracy.
And yeah… that sounds like an exaggeration.
Until you think about how people actually form opinions.
It’s not just through voting. It’s through casual, repeated, low-stakes conversations. Running into people who don’t think like you… and not having the option to mute them.
That kind of interaction needs a physical setting.
It doesn’t happen at home. It doesn’t happen at work. And online… it kind of imitates it, but it doesn’t quite replicate it.
…
Now here’s where things get a bit more uncomfortable.
These places aren’t disappearing randomly.
They’re becoming harder to sustain.
Start with something basic: cost.
Almost every space today is transactional. If you’re not buying something, you’re in the way. If you stay too long, it feels like you’re overstaying.
So the space stops being neutral… and turns into a service.
And once that happens, the dynamic changes completely.
Oldenburg described third places as “levelers”… spaces where status kind of dissolves. (Wikipedia)
But if access depends on money, or on continuous consumption… that equality quietly disappears.
…
There’s another layer that’s harder to notice.
People still go to cafés. Still go to public spaces.
But they’re not really there.
They’re somewhere else, on their phones.
You end up with this strange situation where a place is full… but socially empty. People are physically together, but not interacting. (Wikipedia)
And that’s almost worse than being alone.
Because it creates the illusion of connection without the actual thing.
…
So then the obvious response is: “well, that’s what the internet is for.”
But it doesn’t quite work the same way.
Third places don’t just connect people… they mix people.
You don’t fully choose who you encounter. There’s randomness, friction, unpredictability.
Online, everything is filtered. Curated. Personalized.
Which feels efficient… until you realize what’s missing.
Without friction, there’s no real contrast.
And without contrast… your understanding of other people gets thinner, not deeper.
…
There’s also something subtle about repetition.
Third places were never spectacular. They weren’t “events.” They weren’t content.
They were… boring, in a very specific way.
You go there again and again. You see the same people. Not because you planned it… but because that’s just how the place works.
And over time, something builds.
Today, almost all social interaction requires intention.
You schedule it. You coordinate it. You optimize it.
And slowly… spontaneity disappears.
…
And then you start connecting dots that maybe weren’t obvious before.
Loneliness increasing.
Polarization getting sharper.
That weird feeling of being surrounded by people… but still disconnected.
It’s not that third places explain everything.
But they do feel like one of those invisible pieces holding things together… that we only notice when they’re gone.
…
What’s interesting is that some attempts to “bring them back” don’t really work.
Because they try too hard.
They design them. Brand them. Optimize them.
But third places don’t work because they’re perfect.
They work because they’re ordinary.
Because nothing special is supposed to happen there.
And somehow… that’s exactly why everything happens there.
…
So maybe the question isn’t just why these places are disappearing.
Maybe it’s also whether we still know how to use them… even if they were there.
Because it might not just be a lack of spaces.
It might be that we’ve forgotten how to exist in a place without a purpose.
